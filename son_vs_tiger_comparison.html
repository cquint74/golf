Swing Report – Your Son vs Tiger Woods

Face-on iron swings compared at key checkpoints, plus an actionable drill plan.

Each row compares the same stage of the swing: setup, takeaway, top, transition, delivery, and finish. The right column highlights what Tiger is doing that your son can copy, with emphasis on posture, hip depth, rotation, and controlling early extension.

How to use this: Before each practice, review one row at a time and focus on copying just one Tiger move (for example, setup posture in Frame 1). Then record a few swings and compare again.
Frame Your Son Tiger Woods Comparison Notes
Frame 1
Setup / Address
Your Son
Tiger Woods
Setup Posture & Hip Depth
  • Your son: Stands more upright with less hip hinge; hips are closer to the ball and hands ride a bit high.
  • Tiger: More forward bend from the hips, chest over the ball, hips pushed slightly back creating clear hip depth.
  • Tiger’s arms hang naturally under his shoulders; your son’s are a little more “reaching,” which encourages standing up through the swing.
  • Early extension is often “pre-programmed” at address – copying Tiger’s hip hinge and arm hang here will make the rest of the swing easier.
Frame 2
Takeaway / Early Backswing
Your Son
Tiger Woods
Connection & First Move
  • Your son: Club and arms lift a bit more vertically; trail hip hasn’t really moved back yet, so the pelvis is already inching toward the ball.
  • Tiger: Club, hands, and chest move together; his lead arm stays connected to his chest and the club stays outside his hands.
  • Tiger’s trail hip is beginning to rotate back behind him, preserving hip depth.
  • Goal cue: “Turn everything together and let the right hip move behind you,” copying Tiger’s first move instead of lifting the arms.
Frame 3
Top of Backswing
Your Son
Tiger Woods
Depth, Turn & Arm Structure
  • Your son: Upper body has turned, but hips haven’t rotated as fully; trail leg may straighten without the right hip moving back, reducing available space.
  • Tiger: Upper body and hips are both fully coiled; belt buckle has turned away from the target while maintaining posture.
  • Tiger’s lead arm is across his chest and club shaft is on-plane; your son’s arms may appear steeper or more lifted relative to his chest.
  • The more “around” turn from Tiger makes it easy to shallow the club and rotate through without standing up – exactly what we want your son to feel.
Frame 4
Transition / Start Down
Your Son
Tiger Woods
Pressure Shift vs Hip Thrust
  • Your son: Hips begin to move toward the ball early; spine angle starts to rise and the knees drive toward the ball line.
  • Tiger: Pressure shifts into the lead foot while the hips stay back and begin to rotate; his belt buckle is turning, not jumping toward the ball.
  • Tiger keeps his chest in its tilt while the lower body unwinds – this separation is a huge key to avoiding early extension.
  • Coaching cue: “Shift, then turn” versus “jump toward the ball”; copy Tiger’s feel of rotating around a stable spine angle.
Frame 5
Delivery / Pre-Impact
Your Son
Tiger Woods
Space for the Arms & Club Path
  • Your son: Because the hips have moved closer to the ball, the arms are forced to work more vertically with less room between hands and body.
  • Tiger: Hips have cleared but stayed back, leaving space for the hands to shallow underneath and travel through impact.
  • Tiger’s lead wrist is flatter and clubface more stable; your son may need to “flip” more due to crowding and lack of rotation.
  • Goal: Use the drills to maintain hip depth like Tiger so your son can swing “around” instead of “up and at” the ball.
Frame 6
Post-Impact / Finish
Your Son
Tiger Woods
Rotation Through the Ball
  • Your son: Good natural athleticism; finishes in balance but with earlier loss of posture, so rotation happens from a more upright position.
  • Tiger: Chest and belt buckle are fully rotated toward the target, trail foot released, and posture has been maintained much longer through impact.
  • Tiger’s finish is the result of everything before it: strong setup, deep turn, hips staying back, and continuous rotation.
  • Use Tiger’s finish as a “pose” for your son to copy – if he can hold that balanced, fully rotated finish, the motion leading into it will improve too.

These drills are mapped directly to the frames in the comparison tab. Work on one or two at a time, keeping sessions short and focused.

Drill 1

Butt-on-the-Wall (Frames 1 & 4)

Improves hip depth and prevents early extension.

  • Set up with glutes lightly touching a wall.
  • Rehearse takeaway and backswing while keeping the hips in contact with the wall.
  • In transition, feel the hips rotating while staying “back,” rather than jumping toward the ball.

Key feel: “Turn around the wall, don’t jump off it.”

Drill 2

Chair Behind Hips (Frames 1–5)

Trains rotation instead of thrusting the hips toward the ball.

  • Place a chair just behind the hips at address.
  • Make slow-motion backswings and downswings without slamming the hips into the chair.
  • If he hits the chair on the way down, he is jumping toward the ball instead of rotating.

Key feel: “Clear the hips around the chair, not into it.”

Drill 3

Connected Takeaway (Frame 2)

Matches Tiger’s connected first move.

  • Place a small towel under both armpits.
  • Move chest, hands, and club together for the first 2–3 feet of the backswing.
  • Keep the towel in place as long as possible without squeezing too hard.

Key feel: “Chest and hands move together; arms don’t fly away from the body.”

Drill 4

Lead-Side Rotation (Frames 4–6)

Helps maintain posture and improve rotation through impact.

  • Without a ball, rehearse from the top of the backswing.
  • Feel the belt buckle turning toward the target while the chest stays in its tilt.
  • Finish with chest and hips fully facing the target, in balance.

Key feel: “Belt buckle leads, chest follows – both stay down and around, not up and at the ball.”

Drill 5

Pose the Finish (Frame 6)

Trains sequence, balance, and awareness of a tour-level finish position.

  • Use Tiger’s finish frame as the reference picture.
  • Have him swing at 50–70% speed and hold the finish for a full 3 seconds.
  • Check: weight on lead side, trail foot released, chest and belt buckle at the target.

Key feel: “Finish like Tiger and freeze – if the finish is right, the motion into it will keep improving.”

Weekly structure
  • 2–3 sessions per week, 20–30 minutes each.
  • Pick one frame and one drill per session (for example, Frame 1 + Butt-on-the-Wall).
  • Finish each session with 5–10 “pose the finish” swings.
Progress tracking
  • Re-film the swing every 2–3 weeks from the same face-on angle.
  • Compare to this report and look for improved hip depth and posture through impact.
  • Celebrate visible improvements to keep him engaged and confident.
